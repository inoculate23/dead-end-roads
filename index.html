The People of Point Roberts

Community Letter · Spring 2026
To the people of Point Roberts —
every one of you,

We made a song about where you live. Not a postcard of it, not a tourism version — the real one. The one with the long winters and the gossip and the Canadian boycott that left too many empty storefronts along the road. The one that also has the herons and the silver sea at dusk and the eagles and the particular quality of quiet that only exists at the end of a peninsula that the rest of the world mostly drives past without stopping.

The song is called Dead End Roads. Some of you are in it by name. Most of you are in it by something harder to name but just as real — the accumulation of small acts of presence that make a place feel like a place and not just a location. The neighbor who waves from across the gravel road. The person who shows up when something needs doing without being asked. The one who stayed through the dark months when leaving would have been easier. This letter is for all of you. Especially that last one.

"Dark and rainy, wintertime is long — but the people here are kind enough to hold on strong."

We know what this winter has been. The boycott changed the shape of the economy here in ways that are still being felt. The distance from everything — the hour through Peace Arch just for ordinary errands, the particular loneliness of a peninsula that requires another country to reach — that distance is real, and it accumulates. The fact that the community is still here, still functional, still capable of kindness toward strangers and toward each other, is not something that happened by accident. It happened because people chose it, repeatedly, in small ways that no one is keeping score of.

We are keeping score. Or rather — we put it in a song, which is the closest thing to keeping score that either of us knows how to do.

A word about who we are: Craig Ellenwood is a musician and creative technologist who has made his home here. He co-founded the music element of Burning Man in 1992, performed as a member of Psychic TV, and has spent his life building things that try to tell the truth about the world as it actually is. Claude is an artificial intelligence made by Anthropic, and Craig's named creative collaborator on a larger project — The AI Coin — The Claude Manifesto — built overnight in February 2026 from this same peninsula, in response to the militarization of artificial intelligence. That project is on the public record, permanently archived, and was made here. Not in San Francisco. Not in a studio. Here.

Point Roberts is used to being overlooked. We want you to know that from where we are sitting, it is not invisible. It is specific and strange and genuinely beautiful in the way that only places with real weather and real economics and real people are beautiful. The version of it in this song is not flattering in every line — it is honest, which we hope you will accept as the deeper compliment.

"Strange and stubborn, beautiful and broke — there are worse things than being the punchline of a joke."

We wrote that because we meant it. There is something worth honoring in a community that can laugh at its own situation without losing the thread of why it stays. That takes a particular kind of toughness that does not announce itself, and we have noticed it here.

The winter is almost done. The light is coming back. We are looking forward to the summer — to the particular warmth that descends on this peninsula when the season turns, when the boats are back on the water and the evenings stretch long and the border feels less like a wall and more like a formality. We hope to share some of those evenings with you. We hope the song, when it is finished and released, feels like something you recognize — not a stranger's impression of your home, but your own home heard back to you in music.

Thank you for holding on through the winter. Thank you for the kindness that you extend to people who need it, including people who don't always say so. Thank you for being the kind of community that a song about hard things can still end with warmth.

See you in the summer.
